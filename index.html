NYC Adopts Congestion Pricing

By Arthi Venkatesh

New York City adopted congestion pricing on January 5.

The city began tolling vehicles that cross through New York's central business district, or what the transit authority calls the "Congestion Relief Zone." The area is bounded by 60th St. to the north and the Hugh L. Carey Tunnel to the south.

Source: The Metropolitan Transportation Authority

Vehicles pay different rates depending on the time of day and type of vehicle. For example, taxis or for-hire-vehicles (think Uber, Lyft, etc.) pay between $0.75 and $1.50 per trip, whereas passenger vehicles pay a one time entry charge between $3.50 and $13.50 according to the transit authority.

Congestion pricing has the potential to reduce traffic, reducing travel times and even fuel emissions.

However, it also has the potential to make roads less accessible. The plan's potential economic consequences were part of why New York Gov. Kathy Hochul halted the first iteration of the congestion pricing plan, set to begin in June 2024. The current pricing plan does offer a variety of discounts and exemptions for some traveling through the tolled area — for example, the transit authority offers a discount plan for low-income drivers.

Passing this tolling policy was a difficult road and remains contentious — Fox News reported that New Jersey mayor Steven Fulop has proposed the idea for a reverse congestion toll.

Ultimately, though, congestion tolls may very well take off in the future, offering a more sustainable and efficient source of revenue for public transit and roadway authorities, while also encouraging more sustainable forms of transportation like cycling or walking.
